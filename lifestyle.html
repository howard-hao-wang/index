Dong, M., Bonnefon, J.-F., & Rahwan, I. (2025). Heterogeneous preferences and asymmetric insights for AI use among welfare claimants and non-claimants. Nature Communications, 16, 6973. Open access.

Summary

  • Why it matters: Governments use AI to speed up welfare decisions. Faster processing helps claimants, but accuracy losses can cause unfair denials and false fraud flags.
  • Who differs: In US/UK samples (N = 3,249), the general public tolerates modest accuracy losses for speed; claimants—the primary stakeholders—are less accepting of AI in welfare.
  • Misplaced calibration risk: Non-claimants overestimate claimants’ willingness to accept speed–accuracy trade-offs, even with incentives. Calibrating to aggregate opinion may misalign with those most affected.
  • Quantified trade-off: Participants would trade ~1 week faster processing for a 2.5–5 percentage-point accuracy drop; claimants accept less loss.
  • Trust matters: Acceptance of trade-offs correlates with trust in government.

Implications for policy and design

  • Prioritize stakeholder alignment over majority preferences by directly incorporating claimant voices.
  • Make discretionary choices explicit via model positionality and computational reflexivity documenting data, annotators, and design choices.
  • Use social choice methods to elicit and aggregate diverse feedback when aligning AI behavior.
  • Compare failure modes: AI can be hyper-vigilant and slow to self-correct; humans can be inconsistent or discriminatory. Monitor both.

Reflexivity resources

Cambo, S. A., & Gergle, D. (2022). Model Positionality and Computational Reflexivity: Promoting Reflexivity in Data Science. arXiv:2203.07031

Alignment with diverse feedback

Conitzer, V., Freedman, R., Heitzig, J., et al. (2024). Social Choice Should Guide AI Alignment in Dealing with Diverse Human Feedback. arXiv:2404.10271

Key takeaways

  • Do not equate “public” with “stakeholders.” Claimants’ preferences are distinct and less enthusiastic about AI-led trade-offs.
  • Design for legitimacy: document positionality, adopt reflexive practices, and use principled aggregation of diverse preferences.
  • Track speed–accuracy impacts with claimant-centered metrics, trust, and redress mechanisms.
